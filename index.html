5.1

Hearing & Listening

Linguistic skills of expression involve speaking and writing (sending messages) and listening and reading (receiving them). In oral communication, we spend more time listening than speaking. Yet all kinds of hearing are not necessarily listening — the two are fundamentally different processes.

🔊 Hearing
  • Purely physiological process
  • Sound waves make eardrums vibrate
  • Vibrations travel to the cochlea (inner ear)
  • Converted to electrochemical impulses
  • Brain identifies them as sounds
  • Fast, automatic, requires no effort
  • Cannot be stopped without ear plugs
⟷
🧠 Listening
  • Involves paying close attention
  • Notices combinations and contrasts in speech
  • Brain interprets and infers meaning
  • Requires a decoding system
  • Analyzing, organizing, interpreting sound patterns
  • Deliberate effort required
  • Begins when interpretation & inference begins

The listening process, in short, involves analyzing speech sounds → organizing into patterns → interpreting patterns → understanding by inferring meaning.

5.2

Purposes of Listening

There are various purposes of listening. We commonly listen to matters relating to our interests, attitudes, and beliefs. In the professional world, listening serves a very significant function.

📚 To Learn
🔍 To Increase Understanding
😌 To Relieve Boredom
🤝 Interpersonal Transactions
⚖️ Decision Making
💬 Effective Response
🤝 Formal & Informal Communication
Informal and interpersonal transactions of messages play a significant role in day-to-day work performance. Harmonious personal relationships can be established through effective listening.
⚖️ Decision Making
Listening plays a substantial and definitive role in the process of decision-making. Company executives frequently work upon their listening skills before deciding on a course of action.
💬 Adequate Response
An adequate, relevant and measured response is not possible unless one listens carefully to the stimulus. Listening is the foundation of any meaningful reply.
⚙️
5.3

Factors Affecting Listening

A number of factors affect our habit, method and purposes of listening.

🏛️
Social Role
Listening is influenced by the role we play in society. Professionals like judges, counsellors, psychiatrists and medical practitioners must have special competence in listening. Poor listening would prove detrimental to their efficiency.
💡
Motivation
We listen when we have an idea or expectation of benefiting from it. It is human nature to seek advantages, reward or benefits — at mental, physical, social, or cultural levels.
🧩
Personality
Analytical and thoughtful persons are generally better listeners. Self-centred, aggressive, domineering or argumentative people are usually bad listeners.
🔄
Attitudes
Our attitude toward a matter or person affects our listening. We tend to ignore those we dislike or those with whom we do not have a good rapport.
🏠
Physical Environment
Physical setting plays an important role. Listening is easier in a calm and quiet place than at a noisy location like a railway station.
Key Insight: Listening makes communication happen at two levels — the level of words and the level of feelings. If we do not understand how a person feels, we have not truly understood them. We cannot listen to others unless we first learn to listen to ourselves.
📊
5.4

Levels of Listening

There are primarily three levels of listening, each reflecting a different quality of engagement with the speaker's message.

🟠 Marginal Listening — Lowest Level
Occurs when the speaker's pace is slow, causing the listener's mind to wander. The majority of the message is not heard or understood. The person may hear the words but they hardly communicate any sense. This can lead to lack of understanding and even be seen as an insult to the speaker.
🔵 Evaluative Listening — Middle Level
Occurs when a listener gets free time to evaluate the speaker's message during oral communication. The receiver begins to develop a response contrary to the intent of the message — instead of one idea being shared, two conflicting ideas develop. Particularly problematic when speech is loaded with emotion or threatens the receiver's security or identity.
🟢 Projective Listening — Highest Level
Provides listeners with the greatest potential for effective communication. With an empathetic gesture, the listener projects themselves into the speaker's position to understand what is implied in the speaker's viewpoint. Effective listening must precede any evaluation — understanding comes first. The listener need not agree but must understand. Effective listening is invariably empathetic; it requires listening to feelings as well as words.

5.5

Barriers to Communication

In communication, a number of barriers prevent the achievement of desired results. Three primary categories of barriers are listed below, followed by the four stages at which these barriers can occur.

Barrier 1
🗂️ Lack of Common Frame of Reference
Implies the social and cultural context in which communication takes place. If the context is well defined, both sender and receiver can focus meaningfully on the message.
Barrier 2
😴 Mental & Physical Fatigue
Sometimes one is unable to communicate effectively because of physical fatigue or when psychologically unprepared — due to previous unpleasant experiences in similar situations.
Barrier 3
↔️ The Divergent Messages
The divergence between sent and received messages is increasingly serious as messages get more complicated. Barriers also occur at each successive stage of communication.

🔤 Semantic Difficulties
Arise when the sender uses ambiguous expressions or highly inappropriate vocabulary. Every sender also consciously or unconsciously superimposes their attitudes on what they communicate. A message is not just organized language — it is also a verbal means to realize an intention and attitude.
😤 Differing Attitudes & Fear of Evaluation
When the attitude of sender and receiver clash, communication suffers. Some persons feel reluctant to speak because they are afraid of being evaluated by peers or superiors. Each language has social, regional, professional, and local varieties — using the wrong one causes communication to suffer.

5.6

Communication Barriers in Management

⚙️ Technical Barriers
  • Timing of communication
  • Overloaded information
  • Cultural divergences
🔤 Linguistic Barriers
  • Vocabulary mismatch
  • Semantic differences
  • Jargon & technical terms
🧠 Psychological Barriers
  • Information filtering
  • Lack of trust & openness
  • Jealousy
  • Preoccupation
  • Hearing only what we expect
  • Perception set differences
  • Noise
Technical
⏱️ Timing
Timing of transmitting a message is crucial for its impact. A manager who reprimands a worker six months after an event will likely find the worker has forgotten it entirely.
Technical
📦 Overloaded Information
Occurs when an individual is presented with too much information in too little time. When excessive information is provided, a major breakdown in communication occurs.
Technical
🌍 Cultural Divergences
A 30-minute wait in the US may signal low status; in India it is common; in Japan, long delays are a negotiation tactic. Business distance also varies — Indians typically conduct business at 5–8 feet.
Linguistic
📖 Vocabulary
Every field has its own vocabulary set. Statisticians, skilled mechanics, and unskilled labourers each understand different terms. Using vocabulary beyond the audience's linguistic competence causes communication breakdown.
Linguistic
🔀 Semantics
When a receiver attaches meanings different from those intended while decoding, a semantic lapse occurs. English is particularly prone to this as one word may connote multiple meanings.
Linguistic
💬 Jargons
A jargon is a set of vocabulary items used by members of a specific class related to a specific field. Virtually every industry develops jargon — and jargon for one group may be entirely unfamiliar to another.
Psychological
🔍 Information Filtering
As messages move through an organization, each receiver eliminates certain data while re-encoding it for the next person. This "information filtering" can completely change the original intent of a message.
Psychological
🤝 Lack of Trust & Openness
Managers and employees must trust each other for positive changes. An open and receptive attitude encourages employee feedback. For Japanese managers, success in business is linked to the trust they have in peers and superiors.
Psychological
😒 Jealousy
Performance and accomplishments of a manager may be viewed as a threat by peers and superiors. Because of jealousy, wrong messages may be communicated to upper management.
Psychological
🤯 Preoccupation
People preoccupied with themselves hardly hear the message they are supposed to listen to. They seem absent-minded, causing a loss in the impact and purpose of communication.
Psychological
👂 Hearing What We Expect
We are conditioned to hear what we expect rather than what is actually said. Through years of social and cultural grooming, we develop preconceptions about what is being said and meant.
Psychological
👁️ Perception Set Differences
Everyone has a tendency to interpret information in a certain way (their perception set), shaped by past experience, education, emotions, values, and beliefs. Efficient managers learn to be more analytical and broad-minded to overcome this tendency.
👥
5.7

Barriers to Effective Group Communication

Barriers to communication also affect group communications. Group behaviour, collaboration and competition may also create barriers. Faction-centric perspectives cause hindrances in intergroup communication, polarizing the interacting groups and causing communications to come down to the level of bargaining rather than transmitting facts.

⚔️ Conflicts arising from winning or losing
🎯 Members pursuing their own objectives only
🙈 Members understanding their own needs but concealing them
💪 Members aggrandizing their power and dominance
😰 Yielding to or exercising threats to get submission
📣 Overemphasizing own needs, objectives, and positions
😈 Adopting an attitude of exploiting other members
🏆 Emphasizing differences and one's own superiority
🚫 Isolating other persons or groups

5.8

Techniques to Improve Listening Ability

An analysis of the techniques followed in the process of listening is essential to improve one's listening ability. It starts with developing a desire to learn how to listen — one must analyze their own shortcomings and have a propensity to listen more than to talk.

1
🛡️
Resisting Distractions
Because we think faster than we speak, attention begins to wander. Make a conscious effort to bring the mind back to what is being said. Focus on the message — not the speaker's appearance, delivery, or mannerisms. Be alert to both verbal and non-verbal means of communication.
2
🎧
Focusing on Manner of Listening
Skilled listeners focus on the message rather than the matter of listening. Each speech contains a limited number of points. Adapting to the right manner of listening helps identify these points and the supporting evidence. Evaluate evidence for accuracy, objectivity, relevance and adequacy.
3
⏳
Delaying Evaluation
Give the speaker adequate time to finish. Do not jump to conclusions. First understand the message, then evaluate it. Premature judgement hampers effective listening. Remove mental or emotional blocks. A good listener always keeps their mind open to ideas and information.
4
📝
Taking Notes
Perceiving the essence of speech is not the end of listening — one must also record what is listened to. The technique of note-taking is to acquire main points by writing the key words used by the speaker. Taking notes guides us to become more attentive and creative listeners.
⭐
5.9

Skills of Effective Listening

Effective listening requires energy to concentrate on what is being said, to interpret and understand it, and to evaluate objectively what has been understood. Incompetent listeners do not deploy the right amount of energy — their attention drifts, they follow private side-tracks, or they counter-argue internally.

🎯
Concentration
The art of concentration — largely a matter of attitude. Inculcate the habit of concentrating on the message the speaker is trying to send.
⚡
Mental Alertness
Improved by physical alertness — not just fitness, but how one positions the body, limbs, and head. Self-assess your qualities of competence as a listener.
🔗
Chemistry with Speaker
Developing positive chemistry between listener and speaker helps keep track of the speech and prevents the mind from going wayward. Both parties share responsibility.
👁️
Eye Contact
Proper eye contact is particularly important for interpreting non-verbal clues. Looking at the speaker shows interest. Facial gestures — raised eyebrows, nods, frowns — all make communication effective.
🪑
Posture
An upright posture helps better concentration. Leaning slightly forward shows interest and encourages the speaker. Leaning back expresses lack of interest or a disinterested gesture.
📋
Note-taking
Recommended as an important aid to the listener. Also helps the speaker by giving confidence when listeners take notes. A note-taker paraphrases the speaker's thought in fewer words, summarizing feelings and understanding.
🖼️
Speaker's Frame of Reference
A skilful listener concentrates on the speaker's line of interest and tries to understand the speaker's framework. A nod affirming this frame increases the speaker's sense of being heard and accepted.
❓
Listener's Questions
Questions starting with Do/Have/Will/Is invite yes/no answers. What-questions seek clarification; Why/How questions demand justification. Clarifying questions unify speaker and listener; justification questions can set them apart.
Feedback Loop: Listeners check the accuracy of communication through feedback. A listener who doubts should give their feedback in their own words — reflecting what was understood — rather than simply asking the speaker to repeat. This gives the speaker a chance to confirm or modify the message, ensuring it is clearly understood.
